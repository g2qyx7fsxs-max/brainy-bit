You don't remember being a baby. You looked completely different, couldn't talk, and had none of the interests or memories you have now. So here's a strange question philosophers have argued about for centuries: are you actually the same *person* you were back then?

## A very old thought experiment

Ancient philosophers told a story about a ship — let's call it the **Ship of Theseus**. Imagine a wooden ship that, over many years, has every single one of its planks replaced, one at a time, as they wear out. By the end, not one original piece of wood remains.

Is it still the same ship? Some say yes — it kept the same shape, purpose, and continuous history the whole time. Others say no — physically, literally nothing original is left.

## Your body is doing the same thing

Here's the surprising science: most of the cells in your body are constantly being replaced. Skin cells last a few weeks. Red blood cells last a few months. Over enough years, a huge portion of your physical body has been swapped out — much like the ship's planks.

So if barely any of your original physical material remains from when you were a baby, what makes "you" still you?

## Maybe it's not about the material at all

One common answer: identity isn't really about which atoms make up your body — it's about **continuity**. Each version of you connects to the next one through memory, personality, and an unbroken chain of experience, even though the material itself keeps changing. You today are connected to you-as-a-baby the same way each plank replacement connects to the ship's ongoing story.

> **Quick take:** Almost nothing physical connects you to your baby self — but philosophers argue that identity might be less about matter, and more about an unbroken thread of experience and memory.

## A question to think about

If a robot copied every memory and personality trait you have perfectly, but was built from entirely different material, would that robot be "you"? Why or why not?
